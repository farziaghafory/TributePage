How Horror Stories Help Us Cope With Real Life

Scary movies, books and podcasts can help people think through how they would respond to threats and prepare them for worst-case scenarios, say experts who study the psychology of fear.

One weekend when I was in the fourth grade and my friend Whitney was coming to sleep over, my mother took me to the video store to rent a movie. I picked “A Nightmare on Elm Street” — Mom must not have checked the title or she would have balked — and I loved every second of it. Whitney, on the other hand, didn’t sleep well for weeks. That evening sparked my lifetime love of horror stories. I became the girl who made all her friends watch scary movies at slumber parties. (If you were one of them: I’m sorry.) Stephen King’s “It” remains one of my favorite books. I listen to disturbing true crime podcasts on my daily walks. And I can’t make it through October without watching the original“Halloween.”
